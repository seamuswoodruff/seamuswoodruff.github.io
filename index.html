I'm a Digital and Computational Studies senior at Bowdoin College, studying a major that asks not just what technology can do, but what it should do and who it affects. I've spent four years at the intersection of data analysis, AI ethics, and digital humanities, looking for work where that question actually matters.

Right now, AI is compressing the time between idea and deployment faster than any technology in history. That pace doesn't make people who ask should we? less relevant. It makes them more necessary than ever. The bottleneck is not building quickly. It's understanding what you are building, for whom, and what happens when it scales. That's the work I'm trained for, and the work I want to do.

In practice that means experience in data analysis and NLP pipelines, geospatial visualization, behavioral design research, UX methodology, digital text analysis. Different tools, same question: what is this system actually doing, and to whom? I move between the technical and the interpretive because I think that the problems we are facing as a society do not fit neatly on one side.

I also double-major in Dance, which has expanded how I think about bodies, systems, and what it means to communicate something to another person. Outside the classroom I've worked as a farm intern, a carpenter's assistant, a bike mechanic, and an outdoor guide. That breadth of experience is, I think, exactly the kind of thing that makes someone better at the work of thinking critically about technology, about people, about what we're actually building.
