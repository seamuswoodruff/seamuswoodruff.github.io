01 · Why this study

Built to test one question.

No prior study had made device type the primary research question in AI image detection. We built one.

The gap.

No prior study has directly tested smartphone vs. laptop detection of AI content. The closest existing finding — Sütterlin et al. (2023) — saw mobile viewers significantly less accurate than desktop (mean rank 63.9 vs. 82.9), but that was incidental to a study about political orientation. Other papers either lacked ecological validity or couldn't capture device metadata at all.

Our contribution.

A survey built with device type as the primary predictor. Participants completed the task on their own phone or laptop, in the environment they normally use it. The Bowdoin population holds demographic factors roughly constant — isolating device type as the variable of interest.

Why it matters.

AI content is encountered primarily on phones, via social media. If mobile users are systematically worse at spotting it, that is a direct vulnerability for misinformation spread.

RQ1
What factors affect detection of AI-generated images?
Hypothesis: Greater AI usage and familiarity with AI image generation increases detection ability.
RQ2
Does device type affect ability to detect AI-generated images?
Hypothesis: Viewing on a mobile phone — compared to a laptop — decreases detection ability.
02 · Building the image pool

Existing datasets didn't fit.

Existing datasets were built to train AI detectors, not to test human perception. We assembled a custom set.

No usable datasets.

Existing datasets were built to train AI detectors, not to test human perception. No freely accessible option existed for human-detection studies at the time.

Dataset scale.

Typical datasets hold 10,000+ images per category — far too large for a survey where each participant only sees ~10 images.

Image quality.

Many images were too easy (obvious AI artifacts anyone could spot) or too hard (paintings and artworks requiring prior expertise). Neither suited an average participant.

Source: Kaggle — 6,000 AI-generated + 6,000 real photographs (12,000 total), curated down to 80. kaggle.com/datasets/tristanzhang32/ai-generated-images-vs-real-images

06 · Bias controls

Defended at every stage.

The survey guards against bias at every stage: from instrument design through data cleaning.

Pre-survey Instrument design
  • Acquiescence
    Neutral "real or fake?" wording throughout
  • Experimenter demand
    True hypothesis hidden from participants
  • Order effects
    Demographics collected last; images randomized per participant
  • Fatigue
    Short survey (~4 min); varied question types
During survey Respondent behavior
  • Response bias
    Fully anonymous; raffle entry submitted separately
  • Social desirability
    Reframed as motivation to perform well
  • Ex-ante quality
    Two disguised attention checks embedded in image flow
Post-survey Data cleaning
  • Ex-post quality
    Removed responses failing either attention check or outside ±3 SD of mean response time per image
  • Impact
    508 → 419 responses across three filtering steps; score distribution virtually unchanged (mean 6.95 → 6.98)

09 · The timing variable

Why we log-transformed response time.

Average seconds per image was right-skewed in raw form — a handful of very slow respondents pulled the tail out. We used ln(seconds per image) instead. After log-transforming and trimming ±3 SD, the distribution is approximately normal, making it usable as a continuous predictor in the ordered probit model.

The trim drops 5 respondents and changes the overall score statistics negligibly (mean 6.95 → 6.98). In the final model, log response time did not improve fit enough to justify including it — but the transformation was necessary to evaluate it fairly.
