Finding 01 — Hypothesis 1

How device type affects score

Across the full sample, laptop users averaged 0.53 points higher than mobile users (7.16 vs. 6.63, n = 260 vs. 157, t = 3.36, p = 0.0008). Screen size almost certainly plays a role: a laptop's larger display makes artifacts, lighting inconsistencies, and the subtle wrongness of AI-generated detail easier to catch: detail that compresses away on a smaller screen.

The picture is more complicated at the subgroup level. When the same t-test was run separately for Juniors and First-years, the two class years with the most even device split, neither result was statistically significant (p = 0.51 and p = 0.23 respectively). And in the ordered probit model, which controls for age and demographics, device type alone does not reach significance. The aggregate gap is real, but it is partly entangled with who uses each device, particularly the age effect described in Finding 05.

Finding 02 A

The effect of device type on faculty scores

When the ordered probit model is restricted to faculty and staff (n = 66), device type does not reach statistical significance for any score cutoff. The device gap that appears in the full sample is absent here — consistent with the interpretation that the gap is partly driven by age and experience rather than the device itself.

One structural pattern stands out in the faculty-only model: the cut-point gap between scores 7 and 8 is significantly wider than the others. The probability jump from scoring a 9 to scoring a 10 is the single hardest step for faculty to make — a ceiling effect that doesn't appear as clearly in the student subsample.

Finding 02 B

The effect of device type on student scores

Restricted to students (n = 343), with age, device, and a familiarity × usage interaction, device type reaches statistical significance at scores 9 and 10 (p < 0.05). Using a phone rather than a laptop reduces the probability of scoring a 9 or 10 by 3–4 percentage points.

This is where the device effect lives: not spread evenly across the score range, but concentrated at the top. Mobile users aren't broadly worse — they're specifically less likely to achieve near-perfect detection. Within individual student cohorts the effect doesn't reach significance, because cell sizes become too small to power the test. But across the student body as a whole, the signal is real.

Finding 03 — Hypothesis 2

Effect of AI usage and familiarity on AI image detection

We measured AI familiarity four different ways: a self-rated familiarity scale, a literacy scale, hours per week of AI usage, and whether someone had taken an AI course at Bowdoin. We also tested a familiarity × usage interaction. Across all five familiarity levels, mean scores cluster tightly in the 6–7 range.

Knowing that AI images exist, understanding how diffusion models work, even building with them daily — none of it translates to better detection at the perceptual level. The null result held across all four measures. The eye is not trained by the intellect.

Finding 04

The effects of confidence on how you score

The relationship between confidence and accuracy is real but messier than you'd expect. Highly confident answers (9–10 on the confidence scale) were correct substantially more often than uncertain ones, but the middle buckets reveal something murkier: people were equally confident in wrong answers as in right ones.

The takeaway: your gut is worth trusting when it's very strong, but moderate confidence is nearly meaningless as a signal. The hardest images, the ones that fooled the most people, were often accompanied by high confidence in the wrong direction.

There's also an asymmetry in how people erred. Participants were more consistent at correctly identifying AI-generated images as AI; true positive rates skewed toward the high end. True negative rates, correctly confirming that a real photograph is real, were more spread out and less reliable. When uncertain, people tended to call things AI rather than real. Whether that reflects a general suspicion of imagery in 2026, or bias introduced by the fact that they were taking the survey, is impossible to know.

Finding 05

Response time vs. accuracy

Intuitively, you'd expect careful, deliberate responses to produce better scores. The data disagrees. There's essentially no relationship between how long someone spent on the survey and how well they did.

This suggests that detecting AI imagery is more about perceptual training than analytical effort. You either notice the artifacts quickly or you don't. Spending more time second-guessing yourself doesn't help.

Finding 06

Age vs score

In the ordered probit model, being over 25 is the only demographic variable to reach statistical significance. Participants above that threshold were 3–5 percentage points less likely to score 8, 9, or 10, and 1–2 percentage points more likely to land in the 4–6 range.

The affiliation breakdown makes the pattern visible: Faculty and Staff averaged 6.43/10, the lowest of any group, while students across all four class years clustered between 6.93 and 7.19. The Bowdoin population holds many confounders roughly constant; when age still emerges as a predictor in that context, it suggests that exposure to AI imagery during formative years builds informal visual instincts that older users haven't developed the same way.

Conclusion

A real device gap appears in the pooled sample (laptop ≈ 0.5 points higher, p < 0.001). The age effect is robust: being over 25 measurably reduces the chance of a top-three score. Within students, the device effect survives the model and concretely lowers the probability of scoring a 9 or 10. AI familiarity, by every measure we tried, doesn't help. The next step is a randomised controlled trial that assigns device condition directly — turning this correlational finding into a clean causal estimate. See Future directions.

Limitations

Device-year confound

Device type was not randomly assigned. First-years and Juniors received a mobile-formatted link; Sophomores and Seniors received a laptop-formatted link; Faculty and Staff were split. This means device type is structurally correlated with class year, and therefore with age, by design. Separating the effect of the device from the effect of age and class year is difficult, which is part of why device type loses significance in the ordered probit model once age is controlled for. Residual self-selection also exists: participants could access the survey from any device regardless of the link they received, and word-of-mouth recruits had no device routing at all.

Sample composition

Nearly all participants were drawn from Bowdoin College, a small, highly-educated liberal arts institution. 508 responses were collected; the analytic sample is 419 after three filtering passes: attention-check failures, device-type ambiguity, and response-time outliers (±3 SD). The sample skews young (18–24), academically engaged, and technologically literate relative to the general population. Findings may not generalize beyond similar demographics.

Image difficulty

Our 80 images were not randomly sampled or difficulty-calibrated. Some AI images are visually obvious; others are nearly indistinguishable. The mix shapes our headline numbers in ways we can't fully control for.

Self-reported familiarity

AI familiarity was measured four ways (a familiarity scale, a literacy scale, weekly usage, and AI course enrollment), plus an interaction term. None predicted accuracy. The null result is robust, but all four are still self-reported and subject to inflation.

Survey framing

Participants knew they were in a study about AI detection. Real-world exposure to AI images happens without that framing. The stakes of a quiz may produce different behaviour than casual social-media scrolling, where no one is counting.
